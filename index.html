History!

London, England became a notable place when William the Conqueror decided to use the town as his stronghold.

From there, it grew to be a a center of prosperous trade and commerce.

Today, London is the capital of England, and is rich with its long history through the various dynasties and monarchs who ruled.

Main Attractions/Things to Do!

There are many cool things to see in London. Here are some of them:

Big Ben:
The Houses of Parliament and Elizabeth Tower, commonly called Big Ben, are among London's most iconic landmarks and must-see London attractions.

Technically, Big Ben is the name given to the massive bell inside the clock tower, which weighs more than 13 tons (13,760 kg). It is 105 yards tall (96 meters).

Visit Big Ben for more information!



London Eye:
The London Eye is a giant Ferris wheel on the South Bank of the River Thames in London. It is Europe's tallest Ferris wheel.

The structure is 135 metres (443 ft) tall and the wheel has a diameter of 120 metres (394 ft), and it opened in 2000.

Visit London Eye for more information!



Buckingham Palace:
Buckingham Palace has served as the official London residence of the UK’s sovereigns since 1837 and today is the administrative headquarters of the Monarch.

Although in use for the many official events and receptions held by The Queen, the State Rooms at Buckingham Palace are open to visitors every summer.

Buckingham Palace has 775 rooms, including 19 State rooms, 52 Royal and guest bedrooms, 188 staff bedrooms, 92 offices and 78 bathrooms.

Visit Buckingham Palace for more information!



Shakespeare’s Globe:
Shakespeare's Globe is the complex housing a reconstruction of the Globe Theatre, an Elizabethan playhouse associated with William Shakespeare, in the London Borough of Southwark, on the south bank of the River Thames.

The original theatre was built in 1599, destroyed by fire in 1613, rebuilt in 1614, and then demolished in 1644.

The modern Globe Theatre reconstruction is an academic approximation based on available evidence of the 1599 and 1614 buildings.

Visit Shakespeare Globe for more information!
